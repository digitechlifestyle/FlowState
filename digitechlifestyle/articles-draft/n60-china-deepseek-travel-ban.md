**CONTENT**

China has placed sweeping travel restrictions on artificial intelligence researchers and executives, including those at DeepSeek and Alibaba, requiring government approval before they can leave the country. Confirmed on 26 May 2026, the policy treats AI expertise as a national security asset — a dramatic escalation that has sent ripples through the global technology industry.

The restrictions extend controls previously reserved for nuclear scientists and executives at state-owned enterprises into China's most commercially valuable private-sector AI companies. For the UK tech sector — which competes for the same talent and relies on the same AI tools — this has significant implications.

On the same day the travel restrictions were confirmed, DeepSeek made a 75% price cut to its V4-Pro model permanent. The combination of tighter state control and aggressive pricing tells a complex story about where Chinese AI is heading.

**What China Is Actually Doing**

From 26 May 2026, key AI researchers and company founders at private firms must obtain official approval from government agencies before travelling abroad. The rule applies to named executives and researchers — not all employees — but the scope is broad enough to cover the most strategically valuable people in China's AI sector.

The requirement is framed as a national security measure. China's government argues that top AI talent carries knowledge equivalent in strategic value to weapons scientists or military engineers. Allowing that expertise to leave freely — whether through emigration, foreign acquisitions, or simply conference travel — is now treated as an unacceptable risk.

Multiple sources confirmed the policy in late May 2026, following earlier reporting that some travel restrictions had been quietly enforced since December 2025.

**Why DeepSeek Is at the Centre of It**

DeepSeek is not a typical AI startup. Its parent company, High-Flyer, is a Chinese quantitative hedge fund that pivoted into AI research with remarkable speed. DeepSeek's models — particularly its R1 reasoning model released in January 2025 — caused significant disruption in Western AI markets, demonstrating that high-performance AI could be built at a fraction of the cost of US rivals.

That success made DeepSeek's researchers extremely valuable — and, from Beijing's perspective, extremely vulnerable to poaching or defection. Reports from December 2025 indicated that High-Flyer was already holding certain employees' passports as an informal enforcement mechanism. The formalisation of that approach into a government-level requirement in May 2026 marks a hardening of state control.

DeepSeek's parent company has not publicly commented on the restrictions.

**From Passports to Permission Slips**

The enforcement mechanism matters as much as the policy itself. In December 2025, the restrictions were soft — companies holding passports, informal pressure, unwritten rules. By May 2026, the policy has hardened into a formal pre-approval requirement.

This shift from corporate-level enforcement to government-level enforcement is significant. It means researchers can no longer simply negotiate their way around it with an employer. They must go through a government process — one that can be delayed, denied, or used as leverage — to attend international conferences, visit overseas offices, or accept foreign job offers.

The restriction builds on China's existing export control framework. Since 2023, China has tightened controls on AI-related hardware, algorithms, and training data. The travel restrictions extend that framework to human capital for the first time at this scale.

**How This Compares to Other Restrictions**

Western democracies do not have direct equivalents to this policy. The US has export controls that restrict technology transfers, and CFIUS reviews that block foreign acquisitions of sensitive companies. The UK has the National Security and Investment Act, which allows the government to scrutinise and block foreign investment in sensitive sectors.

But neither the US nor the UK requires scientists or researchers to obtain government approval before travelling. The Chinese approach is qualitatively different — it treats individual knowledge as state property to be managed, not private expertise to be freely deployed.

Some analysts have drawn comparisons to Soviet-era restrictions on scientists, where those working on sensitive projects were classified as "closed" and prohibited from foreign travel. China's restrictions are narrower in scope but follow similar logic.

**DeepSeek's 75% Price Cut — What's Behind It?**

Also on 26 May 2026, DeepSeek made its 75% price reduction on the V4-Pro model permanent. The model, which competes directly with OpenAI's GPT-4 class systems and Anthropic's Claude 3 family, was already among the most competitively priced frontier AI models available.

At these prices, DeepSeek V4-Pro costs significantly less than comparable Western models for most use cases. For UK businesses building AI-powered applications, DeepSeek's pricing makes it an attractive option — but the travel restrictions raise questions about the long-term stability and independence of DeepSeek's research team.

If key researchers are restricted from attending international events, collaborating with overseas institutions, or accessing the global research community freely, innovation cycles could slow. The price cut may reflect a strategy to capture market share aggressively before any such slowdown becomes visible.

**What the West Is Watching**

The restrictions have drawn attention from AI policy observers across Europe and North America. The central concern is not the loss of individual researchers — China's AI talent pool is deep — but what the policy signals about China's approach to the global AI competition.

If China is willing to restrict the movement of its best AI researchers to protect a competitive advantage, it suggests Beijing views the AI race in zero-sum terms: knowledge that stays in China is good; knowledge that crosses borders is a threat.

In the same week, Anthropic and the Gates Foundation announced a joint pledge of $200 million to deploy Claude inside government applications in several countries. That initiative — oriented around open deployment and international collaboration — represents a starkly different model of AI development.

**What This Means for UK AI Businesses**

For UK companies that use or licence DeepSeek models, the immediate impact is limited. The models themselves remain available and the price cut makes them more accessible than ever. API access is not affected by the travel restrictions.

The longer-term risks are harder to quantify. If China's AI researchers become progressively more isolated from international scientific exchange, the quality of research output could diverge from globally collaborative work. UK businesses building products on top of Chinese AI models should factor geopolitical and regulatory risk into their supplier assessments.

For UK AI researchers and policy makers, the Chinese restrictions are also a reminder that talent mobility is a genuine competitive advantage. The UK's open approach to attracting international AI talent — through visas, academic collaboration, and research funding — stands in contrast to Beijing's direction of travel.

The [UK AI Safety Institute](https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/ai-safety-institute) and [DSIT](https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/department-for-science-innovation-and-technology) have not yet commented specifically on the Chinese travel restrictions. But as the geopolitics of AI harden, expect UK policy to adapt accordingly.
